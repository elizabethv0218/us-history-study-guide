Unit 3

Westward Expansion

Foreign Policy

  • During George Washington’s presidency, Americans were forcing Native Americans off their land which prompted the Natives to attack the Americans. President Washington told Congress that the security of the citizens had to be preserved. Although peaceful methods were used to maintain peace (i.e. treaties), they were successful. He believes it necessary to use military forces to push the Natives from their land.

  • The Louisiana Purchase of 1803 doubled U.S. territory and came with new citizens, cultures, and resources. The U.S. paid France $15 million for what would become the West. For Americans, this land meant opportunity, but for the French and Spanish (in America), it was a threat to their culture. The purchase increased the power of President Thomas Jefferson. Fifteen states were formed and they included the states of Kansas, Missouri, and Nebraska.

  • President Jefferson was aware of the concern that expanding U.S. territory would possible risk the nation’s union. Nevertheless, he believed that a larger nation would not be so easily influenced by local issues and that it was best that the West be under their control rather than by another country.

  • John Quincy Adams had established a foreign policy that would prioritize national interests over regional ones (i.e. expanding the nation’s territory).

  • The Monroe Doctrine
    The Monroe Doctrine declared that the Americas were independent and will not be subject to colonization by a European country. Only the U.S. will able to expand in the Americas. The U.S. will be neutral to European wars, unless the interests & rights of the nations are threatened.

Manifest Destiny

  • The Louisiana Purchase made Americans desire westward expansion. “Manifest Destiny” was a belief that Americans had the God-given right to expand the U.S. from the East Coast to the West Coast and spread democracy, regardless of who inhabited the West.

  • Many Americans were attracted by the land that moving to the west would offer. Southern farmers moved to the west because they wanted more land to cultivate more cotton and gain more profits. Miners also traveled to the west when rumors of gold and silver were spread. During this period, the telegraph and railroad were being developed which allowed travelers to continue to travel faster and have a way to communicate if they went to the West.

  • Unfortunately, westward expansion forced Native Americans and herds of animals to leave their land again.

Andrew Jackson and Indian Removal

  • After President Andrew Jackson was elected and the discovery of gold in Georgia, citizens demanded an immediate removal of Native Americans. In 1830, the Indian Removal Act was passed and said that Native Americans who lived southeast of the Mississippi had to move to reservations.

  • In response, the Cherokee claimed the law was illegal. Worcester v. Georgia was a Supreme Court case where the Chief Justice, John, Marshall, ruled in favor of the Native Americans because they were independent and should keep their natural rights.

  • Despite all this, the President overreached his power to continue enforcing the law. As a result, 15,000 Cherokee were moved into reservations through the Trail of Tears. These Natives had to travel on foot during the winter and autumn for around a thousand miles. The duration of the journey was around 115 days and a quarter of them died.

The Mexican-American War

  • Mexican Cession
    • President James Polk’s goal was to expand U.S. territory as he believed in Manifest Destiny. President Polk was among those who wanted land from Mexico.

    • In 1821, Mexico had gained independence from Spain who had controlled them for centuries. Mexico was not able to create a secure government with a prosperous economy and it was this instability that made Americans want to take advantage of Mexico. Mexico had territories that are now southwestern states.

    • So many Americans had settled in Texas that they outnumbered the number of Mexicans who already lived there. The settlers did not want to follow Mexican laws, so they fought to gain independence for Texas in 1836. Mexico was angered when Texas won independence in the Texas Revolution and called itself the Lone Star Republic.

    • The Republic had to deal with a lot of debt and eventually voted to be annexed by the U.S. Mexico believed that the U.S. had violated a treaty and both nations disagreed over where the border was.

    • Because Mexico was not willing to sell more land, President Polk decided war was the answer. American troops were sent to land claimed by both countries, and Mexico followed suit. Polk persuade Congress to declare war when he heard that Mexican troops fired first. The Mexican-American War ended in 1847, when U.S. troops seized Mexico City. Mexico had to agree to ceding almost a million acres of land and a new border was negotiated upon in the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo. The U.S. gained territory that is known as the states of California, Colorado, Utah, Arizona, Nevada, Wyoming, and New Mexico. The Mexican government received $15 million dollars for the land.

The Oregon Territory & The Gadsden Purchase

  • Great Britain and the U.S. claimed authority over Oregon country. Both countries agreed on a joint occupation treaty (1818) that allowed citizens from both countries to equal access on the region. The American population in Oregon country was greater than that of the British population and American settlements were spreading. They wanted the U.S. government to gain complete control over the territory and President Polk was willing to go to war. England was not willing to engage in war because the territory was far from the home country. The Buchanan-Pakenham Treaty moved the border between the U.S. and British Canada towards the west.

  • Antonio Lopez de Santa Anna was Mexico’s leader and had to manage many rebellions, so he sold some land to the U.S. to pay his army to end the rebellions. The U.S. obtained land that is known as Arizona and New Mexico for $10 million.

The California Gold Rush

  • In 1848, traces of gold were found in Sierra, Nevada. This discovery had spread throughout the world and thousands of people traveled to California in search of gold. California migrants, “Forty-niners” were impulsive when they abandoned their farms, jobs, homes, even their families. The society they made in California was tense because most of the migrants were white men.

  • In 1848, traces of gold were found in Sierra, Nevada. This discovery had spread throughout the world and thousands of people traveled to California in search of gold. California migrants, “Forty-niners” were impulsive when they abandoned their farms, jobs, homes, even their families. The society they made in California was tense because most of the migrants were white men.

  • Emigration brokers would give loans to migrants so they could travel to California which were to be repaid from the earnings of migrants.

  • A labor shortage was created and that opened up many job opportunities in California. In fact, it was the people who sold resources to the miners that actually gained wealth. There weren’t many goods in California, so they became expensive.

  • Wealthy people were more likely to get gold because they could afford the advanced technology that would mine them. Most of the Forty-niners did not find gold. Some would go back home and others simply stayed. San Francisco's population had increased by thousands and in diversity after the Gold Rush.

New Technology during the Gold Rush

  • The Transcontinental Railroad
    The Transcontinental Railroad was a means of transportation to move goods from one coast to the other. It greatly expanded the nation’s economy. The Railroad had people believe that the West was the “land of promise”. However, its construction required the U.S. to push the buffalos from their habitat and a lot of hard labor from the Chinese. It also produced pollution.

  • The Erie Canal
    The Erie Canal is a canal in New York that dreww eastward many of the midwestern crops & foods. The canal was an economic success because transportation of goods was more efficient. In the next generation, it led to Manifest Destiny.

New Acts

  • The Homestead Act
    The Homestead Act allowed people to purchase plots of 160 acres of land for a small fee if they occupied said land for 5 years and worked to improve it. It impacted migration by having people believe new markets of commercial agriculture could be created for the economy. People could finally purchase land in the West.

  • The Dawes Act
    The Dawes Act promoted the assimilation of Native Americans into white American society. Natives were made to believe that being more privileged if you act white. In that way, they would have a greater chance at owning land.
