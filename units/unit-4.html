Unit 4

Slavery & War

Technology in Antebellum America (1789-1861)

  • The Southern economy grew with trade of staple crops: sugar, rice, tobacco, and cotton. They were a major force in international commerce and tied it to the emerging capitalist world of American and European partners.

  • Tobacco was an unstable trade system and exhausted the land.

  • Rice was a stable trade system and lucrative, but it required a lot of irrigation and a long growing season in a small area.

  • Sugar required a lot of labor and growing time.

  • Cotton seeds were difficult to remove but the cotton gin solved that issue. Eli Whitney created the cotton engine.

  • The South had a smaller transformation that the North, so it remained agrarian.

  • The South's plantation system depended on slave labor which compromised its economy.

  • In the northeast, people manufactured goods instead of doing agriculture. Francis Cabot Lowell built the Lowell Mill to spin and weave cotton. Textile factories and mills were created. The most profound economic development was the rise of the factory.

Northwest Ordinance

  • Ordered that new western territories could become states that would be treated as equals to existing states and that slavery would be banned from the western region, north of the Ohio River. The population of a territory had to reach 60,000 free inhabitants to create a state constitution and apply to Congress for statehood.

  • In 1819, the U.S. had 11 free and 11 slave states, so they had equal power.
    The Missouri territory was the first state west of the Mississippi river. The North wanted to ban more slaves in Missouri. Southerners wanted for expand their trade of selling slaves. They worried that if it were a free state the Senate would be against slave states.

  • When Maine applied for statehood, the North had a bigger population. They had 148 seats in the House of Representatives but slave states only had 82. The Senate was balanced in terms of Senators. Then California applied for statehood, after the Gold Rush, and wanted to be a free state.

Arising Issues

The U.S. discovered the vast markets of Asia and trade grew with China and Japan. Merchants and manufacturers wanted a transcontinental railroad to connect the East and West.

The South wanted a southern route across the territories gained from Mexico. The North wanted Chicago to be the Midwest hub.

In 1854, the government wanted to take back the land they gave to Native Americans which were called the Kansas and Nebraska territories. North believed that since those territories were in the North, there shouldn’t be slavery. The South said that, because there's more free states, they should be a slave state.

Nat Turner's Rebellion

Nat Turner was an American slave who led a slave rebellion in 1831. It was composed of trusted slaves and he gathered supporters in Southampton County, Virginia. They went house to house, freed slaves, and killed whites who found out. 55 men, women, and children were killed. Rebels included more than 50 enslaved & free blacks.

On Nov 5, 1831, he was tried, convicted and sentenced to death. After being hung on the 11th, his body was flayed, he was beheaded and then quartered - pulled apart by arms and legs.

200 blacks, most of whom were innocent, were murdered by white mobs. Slaves as far as North Carolina were accused with being involved and were tried and executed.

Henry "Box" Brown Escapes the North

Henry Brown was a born slave in Louisa County, Virginia. He fell in love with a slave, Nancy, and asked to marry her. He paid her owner to keep her under his care and to rent a house for their family of 5. Lee, the owner, sold his wife and children into slavery in North Carolina. Brown’s own owner didn’t help him get his family back. His friends put him in a box and shipped him to the Pennsylvania Anti Slavery Society in Philadelphia. Then he went to Massachusetts and spoke at an antislavery rally in Boston.

John Brown's Raid at Harpers Ferry

Brown believed God chose him to end slavery.

  • Kansas Nebraska Act states that popular sovereignty decides whether or not slavery would expand into new territories.
    So he and his followers went to Kansas, dragged and killed 5 pro-slavery people with swords in front of their families.

  • The Scott v. Sanford Supreme Court decision said that slaves were property and not U.S. citizens which enraged Brown.
    So he and his men took control of an arsenal to supply slaves with weapons for a slave revolt. U.S. troops surrounded the arsenal, killed half of his men (including his 2 sons), and others were captured. This was a part of Bleeding Kansas.

  • Bleeding Kansas was a series of violent political confrontations between pro and anti-slavery supporters.
    Brown was convicted and sentenced to death. People believed he was a terrorist trying to end slavery. Others say he was a Freedom Fighter against an unjust government.

Harriet Tubman's Underground Railroad

Anti-slavery supporters had a network of people who would assist fugitive slaves in their escape, known as the Underground Railroad. The "conductors":

  • hid runaways in secret tunnels & false cut boards;
  • gave them food & clothes; and
  • escorted them to the next "station" in disguise.

Tubman was a conductor. She made 19 trips to the South and helped 300 slaves, including her parents, flea to freedom.

Escaping was dangerous because people had to travel on foot at night without a sense of distance or direction, except for the North Star and other natural signs. This meant avoiding patrols of armed men on horseback and struggling through the forest, across rivers, and from starvation. These people were free when they were in Canada.

Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass

Douglass was an escaped slave who became an activist author and speaker. He was taught to read and write by one of his master's wives and he committed himself to learning more and staying politically informed especially with the Abolitionist Movement. He was free on his second attempt to freedom. He found out that his master didn’t want him to learn because he would outwit him and gain more power.

The Fugitive Slave Act

The Fugitive Slave Act of 1850 made it easy to legally seize enslave any black man or woman at large. When a slave escapes to another territory or state the slave owner may pursue & reclaim the slave and use any reasonable force & restraint necessary to take & remove such fugitive person back to where they escaped from. A slaves testimony will not be admitted as evidence in a trial or hearing.

Anyone who knows or prevents a slave owner, his agent or attorney, from arresting a fugitive or attempt to rescue a slave from the custody of the slave owner’’s shall be subject to a fine of at most $1,000 and imprisonment not exceeding 6 months.

A white man only had to swear ownership of the black person and request a certificate for arrest. The law gave Commissioners greater payment if they ruled in favor of the slave owner. $10 for issuing a certificate and $5 for refusing it. It was an era of slave hunting and those who escaped fled to Canada.

Abolitionists tried to undermine the Fugitive Slave Act through legal tactics, social protest or violent resistance and most northern states intentionally neglected to enforce the law. Free blacks were kidnapped by bounty hunters and sold.

Dred Scott v. Sanford

Dred Scott was a Missouri slave and his owner took him into Illinois and Wisconsin where slavery was forbidden. His owner died and Scott sued the Master's widow for freedom because his residence in free territory had liberated him from slavery. The county court declared him free.

The widow’s brother claimed ownership of Scott and told the state supreme court to reverse the decision because Scott had no standing to sue since he was not a U.S. citizen.

Chief Justice Roger Taney agreed. He said slaves had no claim to own citizenship and that under the Fifth Amendment, since slaves were property, Congress was prohibited from taking property without due process of law.

Abolitionists in Antebellum America

The Abolitionist Movement comprised of organized groups of white and black people who wanted to end slavery before the Civil War. They made speeches and written works such as pamphlets, posters, newspapers, and books.

  • Harriet Beecher Stowe and Uncle Tom's Cabin
    There was a powerful religious set of ideas during the Abolitionist Movement. Stowe helped runaway slaves and detested the Fugitive Slave Act. Uncle Tom's Cabin was a success selling 300 copies in the U.S. and more than a million in Great Britain at the end of its first year. It revealed the brutal realities of slavery that harmed everyone associated with it and it enraged slaveholders.

  • Sojourner's Truth
    After her owner went back on a promise to emancipate her, Truth escaped with her infant daughter. In 1827, New York freed all enslaved people. She went to court to recover her son and she became the first black woman to win the case against a white man. She toured the country giving speeches about justice & equality and became an abolitionist leader. She wanted American society to recognize black women's capabilities and demand for their equal standing.

  • William Lloyd Garrison
    Started his newspaper called The Liberator. Its purpose was to educate people about the cruelty of slavery. It helped establish the American anti-slavery society and serves as its president. He was the most well-known abolitionist in the U.S. and slave owners despised him. In fact, Georgia legislature placed a $5,000 bounty on his head and he received numerous death threats from white Southerners, but he remained committed to fighting for an end to slavery.

The Election of Abraham Lincoln

Political social and economic differences over slavery continued to divide the country.

  • The South wanted pro-slavery candidate.
  • The North wanted anti-slavery candidate.
  • The West wanted a candidate who supported popular sovereignty.

  • Lincoln hated slavery but was not an abolitionist. He didn't want to force an end to slavery, but he did want to ban it in new Western territories. He did not hate slave owners. He believed black people were entitled to all natural rights and that slavery was a greater wrong.

  • On November 6th he won the presidential election. He had 36% of the popular vote, the smallest ever, and a clear majority of 180 votes in the Electoral College.

  • Electoral college is a system in which a President is elected not by Congress but by electors chosen by the people and local elections number of electors vary depending upon the combined number of congressional representatives and US senators. It was a compromise between those wanting the president elected by a vote in Congress and those preferring a direct democracy.

  • He became president without winning one single electoral vote from the South! The South was outraged.

South Carolina's entire delegation of U.S. congressmen and Senators resigned. On December 28, 1860 all 169 delegates to the convention voted for secession.

Fire Eaters

Fire Eaters urged secession in 1860 and were a pro-slavery group.
They believed they were criticized as tyrants and heathens. They didn't like that schools taught their children to view the slave owner as the devil's disciple. They didn't like that the North sent troops to Kansas to cut the throats of all slave owners and ambassadors to motivate their slaves to rebellion against their masters because that endangered their lives and would lead to the destruction of their property. They were upset that the North had repealed the Fugitive Slave Law and not followed the decision of the Supreme Court guaranteeing their right to claim our property. That's why they believe they have no choice but to cut off all communication with the North and treat them as an enemy. They did not want to submit to the North because they were a “tyrant brother”. The South could not wait to be a free and independent people.

Mathew Brady Documents the Civil War

  • The northern advantages were having more money, men, industry, and railroads.

  • Southern advantages included confidence, discipline from the famous military school West Point United States Military Academy, and bravery.
    The Union blocked Confederate ports which made the sale of Kyon or receives more difficult. Farms and industries were robbed of a male Workforce. In the North production of all Goods increase and in the South it declined by more than 1/3.

  • The union started using the tactic of Total War which is the use of all available resources (human industrial agricultural military technological and natural) to completely destroy an enemy beyond the ability to resist.

    The North attacked and killed Southern civilians. They also burned farms, cities, railroad tracks and demolished as much of the South as possible. As expected the South eventually surrendered. More than 350,000 Union troops lost their lives and more than 250 Confederate troops.

    Brady Matthew hired photographers to follow the troops to the battlefields. He was determined to document the war. He photographed corpses which brought the terrible reality and intensity of War to the public. His photographs include:

    • Corpses on the battlefield near a plantation;
    • Ruins and debris of one person laying dead after the North attacked a cotton plantation;
    • A burned building from Total War;
    • An old white man sitting next to his rifle to protect himself; and
    • People trying to finding skeletons

The Emancipation Proclamation

President Abraham Lincoln's Emancipation Proclamation was an executive order that liberated Africans and African Americans from slavery.

Freeing slaves would quieten the uproar of abolitionists and strengthen the nation's moral. However, it would antagonize the border states (Delaware, Maryland, Kentucky, and Missouri) and Northern Democrats who only wanted to preserve the Union.

As the northern Army push southward fugitives went across the Union lines. Contrabands were illegally imported goods and if slaves were found to be in contrabands, they were freed. Freed slaves have them work with reasonable wages and were received into the Armed Forces if they chose to do so because it was a military necessity.

Frederick Douglass Responds to Lincoln's declaration
Lincoln worried that Northerners who opposed the war would force him to accept an early peace which would leave all those who have not escaped in slavery. This was thought to have been considerate. Frederick Douglass believed that the Proclamation was not just a necessity.

Aftermath of the Civil War

  • Over 600,000 men were dead and more were crippled and traumatized for life.
  • One of the longest wars and the bloodiest war in American history before or since.
  • The Confederate Army leader Robert E Lee and Ulysses S Grant leader of the Union Army arranged a Confederate surrender. Within a month, Confederate resistance collapsed.

  • Lincoln expanded the power of the executive branch by sending troops into battle without asking Congress for a declaration of war because it was a domestic Rebellion, he increased the size of the army without legislative Authority, ordered military arrest of civilians who were drafted to the war and suspended the right of habeas corpus (the right to an American person to receive a speedy trial), and those who discouraged enlistments were also subject to martial law (military control over civilians).

  • North's economy was strengthened due to a spur and Industry and railroad development.

  • South's economy was weakened by destroying millions of dollars of property and the feeding the Region's young male population.

  • The war led to a growing Northern dominance. Hundreds of thousands of slaves literally freed themselves and deserted their masters and seeked Refuge behind Union lines.

  • The South was a grim place because towns had been gutted, plantations burned, fields neglected, Bridges and railroads destroyed.

  • White Southerners face starvation and homelessness.

  • Southern blacks had freedom but nowhere to go and few possessions.

  • Lincoln offered a general pardon to white Southerners who pledged loyalty and accepted the abolition of slavery. When 10% of the state's total voters took oath in 1860 they could make a state government.

  • Lincoln proposed the right to vote to African Americans who were educated, owned property or had served in the Union army.

  • Further plans that Lincoln had to unify the nation were never implemented. 5 days after Lee surrendered Lincoln and his wife went to Ford's Theater to see a comedy and the man crept up behind Lincoln and shot him in the back of his head. he died on April 15th it was the first time a president had been assassinated. Slavery and secession were no more and the Civil War had ended. now they just needed to restore the southern states to the union and integrate the newly freed African-Americans.
