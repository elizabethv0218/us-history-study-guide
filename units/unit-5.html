Unit 5

Reconstruction

President Lincoln’s Plan for Reconstruction

President Lincoln wanted the Civil War to end immediately and believed that his plan would make the Confederacy surrender. His Ten-Percent Plan states that:

  • A pardon will be granted if the Confederacy makes an oath of loyalty to the U.S. and obey all federal laws that concern slavery.

  • Confederate officials and military leaders will be excluded from the above process temporarily.

  • A state will be allowed to establish a new government and elect representatives for Congress when 10% of voters who voted in the 1860 election make their oaths.

  • Southerners would have their private property protected (this did not include their slaves).

Presidential Reconstruction Plan

After President Lincoln’s assassination, Vice President Johnson became President. He believed in state’s rights and that the government could not command Reconstruction. His plan says that:

  • Pardons will be granted through oaths.

  • The wealthiest Confederates would have to apply for a pardon in person to the President.

  • African Americans will be given equal rights if a state wants to grant them. Southern governments should not be forced by the government to accept laws about freedmen. A state will decide whether freedmen are given an education, money, jobs,etc.

  • African Americans were disenfranchised unless they could read and write.

Radical Reconstruction Plan

The Radical Reconstruction Plan was developed by Thaddeus Stevens who was a Radical Republican. Radical Republicans supported the punishment of the South and having freedmen be protected by the law and government. Their plan says that:

  • A convention will be called to write a new Constitution.

  • African Americans will be guaranteed full rights and the right to vote.

  • Confederate officials and military officers will not be able to vote or hold political office.

  • Lands owned by Confederate supporters will be taken, divided into farms, and given to freedmen.

  • Confederate states will pay the war debt.

  • The military will rule the Southern states until new laws are enforced.

The Reconstruction Amendments

With Reconstruction came issues concerning the social, political, and economic status of freedmen. The Civil War Amendments were created to have the entire male population of America be given their rights.

  • The Thirteenth Amendment (1865) says that slavery and involuntary servitude shall not be legal, unless it is as a punishment for a crime. Therefore, people who work must give their consent and be paid.

  • The Fourteenth Amendment (1868) says that people who are born or naturalized in the U.S. will have the privileges or immunities of U.S. citizens, they will not be denied of their natural rights, and will be protected by U.S. laws. The Fourteenth Amendment also establishes the idea of citizenship & equal protection under the law. This is the first time in which African Americans are viewed as people rather property.

  • The Fifteenth Amendment (1870) says that U.S. citizens will have the right to vote regardless of their background. Consequently, Congress gains more power and citizens are not meant to be discriminated against. However, this amendment does not address women.

The Freedmen’s Bureau (1865)

Immediately after the Civil War, troops were sent to the South to protect newly freed African Americans. Congress established the Freedmen’s Bureau to provide food, schooling, medical care, and clothes to freedmen. The Bureau was made in an effort to help African Americans to settle in their own land through labor contracts with white landowners. Freedmen were also assisted by regaining the connection with family members who were separated and to legalize marriages. Unfortunately, the Bureau only operated for a year which was not enough time to resolve the all problems in the South. White resistance made the Bureau fail to help freedmen settle in their land.

  • The Freedmen’s Bureau School were free and taught African Americans crafts & skills for economic independence. Women learned how to sew and men learned trading skills.

  • Congress began to have black members. After the 15th Amendment, African Americans tried running for different elected positions. They were able to have a say in making changes in the U.S. government.

  • Black owned businesses that served their communities rose African Americans into the middle class - a sign of social mobility. This was necessary because white owned businesses didn’t want to collaborated with African Americans.

  • African American families and people would move to the West to obtain land because they were able to buy land at cheap prices and farm for themselves.

Unreconstructed Southerners

  • Many white southerners were resentful towards the result of the Civil War and they resisted efforts of reconstruction. At this time, President Johnson was finding loopholes in the Reconstruction Amendments and passing laws that would deny the civil rights of African Americans.

  • Black Codes
    Eventually, all southern states passed Black Codes that limited the freedom of African Americans.
    • African Americans had to sign work contracts with working conditions that were similar to those of slavery. They would be arrested if they couldn’t prove that they were employed and be sentenced to a year of labor without being paid.
    • African Americans were not allowed to bear arms.
    • African Americans could only rent property in cities.

  • Apprentice Laws
    The Apprentice Laws in Mississippi and South Carolina required orphaned children and children with unfit parents (deemed by the Probate Court) to find work as an apprentice with former owners having top priority. Their masters could inflict punishment and recapture them if they ran away. The masters were required to provide their apprentices food, clothing, and schooling.

  • Republicans were angered by the Black Codes because the South was regressing. In return, the Civil Rights Act of 1866 was passed to give African Americans equal legal rights as white Americans. President Johnson vetoed the act because it discriminated against white people and gave the government too much power. Regardless, his veto was overridden by Congress for the first time in history and he lost support.

  • Because Johnson did not support Reconstruction, Congress limited his power with a law that prevented the president from removing officials from the cabinet without the approval of the Senate. When President Johnson fired his secretary of war the House of Representatives voted to have him impeached. A trial in the Senate followed, but Johnson was not convicted by one vote. Nonetheless, his power was greatly weakened.

System of Sharecropping

Freedmen wanted land of their own to gain economic independence. Although the Freedmen’s Bureau had helped them gain land, President Johnson had reversed those efforts. Tens of thousands of freedmen had to return to their land to white owners. Freedmen had to become farmworkers to survive in the system of sharecropping. This system basically re-enslaved African Americans because they couldn’t leave their work without permission from landowners. If they left, they were left homeless, jobless, and arrested. Sharecropping was a cycle of poverty as shown below:

  1. Sharecropper is given land & seed and promises to give half the crop to the landowner.

  2. Sharecropper buys food & clothing from landowner’s store.

  3. Sharecropper will plant and harvest crop.

  4. The landowner is given crop to sell. Sharecropper receives half the earnings but pays his debt to the landowner for the year.

  5. As the sharecropper settles up, the landowner will say that more money is owed in comparison to his earnings.

  6. The sharecropper promises to give the landowner a greater share of next year’s crop to pay the debt.

  7. Repeat Steps 1-6.

The Ku Klux Klan

  • The Klu Klux Klan was an organization of white supremacists who were set on inflicting terror & violence on black Southerners. Their goal was to intimidate them in order to influence elections.

  • Abram Colby was a victim of the KKK. In his testimony, he says that he was taken out of his home into the woods to be whipped for 3 hours. They didn’t want him to vote for the Radical party. Those KKK members had a high status and weapons. Before the incident they had offered him $7,500 to comply with their orders, but he refused. They even threatened his daughter at gunpoint. They did not want him to be elected, so they “peppered” his house with bullets. Colby is traumatized.

Carpetbaggers and Scalawags

  • Republicans were becoming stronger in the political world because of African American voters, but they were disliked by white southerners. These southerners referred to Republicans who worked in southern state governments as “carpetbaggers” and “scalawags”.

  • In the eyes of unreconstructed southerners, carpetbaggers were opportunistic who brought cheap suitcases to the South to gain power. However, those people were simply Union soldiers & veterans, teachers, social workers, and ministers who wanted to help improve the devastated South.

  • Southern Democrats called southern Republicans scalawags and viewed them as greedy traitors. Southern Republicans had supported the Union during the Civil War as small farmers.

Jim Crow Laws

White southerners wanted to separate the races in America and prevent African Americans from voting. They found loopholes in the Fifteenth Amendment to disenfranchise African Americans. Jim Crow laws were created and could apply to someone who was more than an eighth African American.

Voting

  • A poll tax or a for of property qualification was used to disenfranchise African Americans. Every vote had to be paid with an annual tax. Most African Americans could not afford to pay this tax, so they couldn’t vote.

  • Literacy tests were also required because it would prove that African Americans were capable of interpreting the U.S. Constitution. Most freedmen were illiterate because of laws that prevented slaves from learning, therefore it was not easy to pass the already difficult tests. The black vote had decreased substantially.

  • White southerners who did not pass the literacy tests or couldn’t pay the poll tax had another chance to vote through the grandfather clause. This clause said they were allowed to vote if he, his father, or grandfather had been able to vote before January 1, 1867. Freedmen had not been emancipated at that point, so they couldn’t vote until after 1870.

Segregation

Jim Crow laws established a system of segregation in the South. Blacks and whites could not share public institutions like waiting rooms, bathrooms, restaurants, theatres, etc. These laws also removed the social, political, and economic advantages they previously obtained. White violence, like lynching by white mobs, and the Jim Crow laws prevented African Americans from receiving equal rights.

Plessy v. Ferguson (1896)

  • In 1890, Louisiana had adopted the system of segregation on its railroads. In 1892, Homer Plessy sat in the “Whites Only” car of the train. The Citizens’ Committee of New Orleans had Plessy intentionally violate Louisiana’s law because he was an eighth black. When Plessy refused to move, he was arrested, tried, and convicted. Plessy appealed and said that he had been denied protection under the law which was stated in the Reconstruction Amendments.

  • The Supreme Court ruled against Plessy because the state’s law on segregation did not violate the 14th Amendment. In other words, separate treatment did not mean that African Americans were inferior. They also said that the quality between the White Only car and Jim Crow car was pretty similar. Justice Marshall Harlan disagreed with the ruling and said that the Constitution should be “color-blind”. He believed the court ruling has committed and disguised a wrong.

Booker T. Washington

Booker T. Washington wanted other African Americans to embark on a journey of self-improvement. His skills as a politician and speaker managed to gain the support of northern and southern whites who donated money to the education of freedmen. He believed his race would be prosperous if they worked their way up through education and explored different fields of work. He wanted African Americans to comply with the white man’s wishes, like segregation, for the time being and later achieve a higher status.

W.E.B. DuBois

W.E.B. DuBois was vehemently against Washington’s plan to comply with white interest. He believed that African Americans had to be defiant and go against the Jim Crow laws. DuBois did not want freedmen to surrender their political power, civil rights, or wealth because doing so will bear no fruit for the community; it will only harm it. Freedmen must demand their rights, equality, and education to be leaders. Only then will progress and change occur.
