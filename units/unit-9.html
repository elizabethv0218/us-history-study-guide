Unit 9

WWI & Aftermath

US Involvement in WWI

  • The US supplied the Allied powers with war supplies.
  • They wanted to ensure Allied repayment of debts.
  • They wanted to prevent Germany from threatening US shipping.
  • German U-boats sank the Lusitania, a British boat that held 128 American passengers.

American Opinion on Joining WWI

  • Americans should be proud to fight for their country.
  • Americans do not want their family members to die/mothers do not want to lose their sons.

Immigrants in America During WWI

  • People should only speak in English in case they are spies. People will be sent to jail or fined if they do not comply.
  • Anti-war activities will not be tolerated and people will be fined or put in jail.
  • Schenck v. United States
    • Charles Schenck was a Philadelphia socialist that organized the distribution of 15,000 pamphlets to convince men to not join the army. He correlated the draft with involuntary servitude, which goes against the thirteenth Amendment. He was arrested and his case went to the Supreme Court. He argued that is First Amendment rights were violated, but he was found guilty because he violated the Sedition Act.
    • The Espionage and Sedition Acts was a federal law that mandates that citizens can’t interfere with military recruitment/banned disloyal speech in America.
    • Clear and Present Danger: The Supreme Court determines under what circumstances and limits can be placed on the First Amendment freedom of speech, press or assembly.

Leagure of Nations

  • Leagure of Nations was an organization for international cooperation established by the Allied Powers at the end of WWI.
    • President Woodrow Wilson hoped that the League of Nations would stop war from happening and countries should not interfere with another's independence.
    • Opposition of Creation
      • William Borah, a republican US senator, was afraid that war will result from the League of Nations.
      • Henry Cabot Lodge, a republican US senator, believed the US will be more powerful without the League of Nations because the country will become tied to the interests and fights between other nations.
    • Wilson’s Points of Peace outlines ways to bring peace and end WWI
      • 1: Peace should be discussed publicly
      • 2: Oceans are the property of no one
      • 3: Countries who agree to peace and have independence have the right to open and free trade
      • 4: Lower the amount of military weapons to only what is necessary
      • 5: People should be able to choose what they want to do
      • 10-13: Austria-Hungary, Romania, Serbia, Montenegro, Turkey, and Poland can be their own independent countries
      • 14: The League of Nations will keep all countries independent

The Red Scare

  • The Red Scare was a communist scare that occured in the US in 1919.
    • Causes:
      • The US was scared that communists would take over the US.
      • 36 bombs were sent out to important political people, one exploded on Wall Street.
      • The Bolsheviks took over Russia.
      • Spreading of rumors
      • Mass paranoia and hysteria
    • “Reds” = symbolic for communists. Their goal was to abolish capitalism everywhere.
    • A Communist Party formed in the US and 70,000 radicals joined.
    • Sacco and Vanzetti were Italian immigrants who were accused of the robbing and murder of a factory pay master. They were found guilty with no circumstantial evidence. They acted as scapegoats.

The Great Migration

  • The Great Migration was a period when six million African-Americans moved North because of the lack of job opportunities in the South.
    • Pull Factors:
      • African-Americans started to be enticed to move North because after WWI, there was a shortage of industrial workers.
      • The Harlem Renaissance was the cultural, social, and artistic rebirth of African-Americans. They made their own community in Harlem where they can live together and understand each other.
      • Economic opportunity in the North
    • Push Factors:
      • Segregation, Jim Crow laws, and lynching in the South still existed.
      • There was no economic opportunity in the South.

The Roaring Twenties

  • The Roaring Twenties was a period when six million African-Americans moved North because of the lack of job opportunities in the South.
    • New technology made life easier (cleaning supplies, cars… )
    • Increase in advertisements
    • Creation of buying things on credit, then pay small amounts each month
    • More people investing in the Stock Market
    • People are spending more money because incomes increase and they don’t know what to do with their extra money.
    • Working hours decreased, which gives people more leisure time
    • People’s standards of living increases
    • Consumption and Production of the Twenties
      • Mass Produced Vehicles
        • Assembly line made production faster
        • Ford Model-T was the first car made for the middle class
      • Refrigerator and dishwasher
        • Food could be stored for longer and easier
        • Involved less physical labor
      • Consumer Credit
        • Pay small portion upfront and pay the rest with installments
        • Allowed the middle class to enjoy a level of luxury and comfort that was before only had by the wealthy
        • People can have things faster/right away and they don’t have to save up to pay all at once
      • The Radio
        • Provided direct entertainment
        • Easier to gain information from other places of the country like weather and the daily news
        • Mass media was born
        • Commercial breaks were created
    • Women In The Twenties
      • Flappers were women who made more social change by wearing tight clothes and boyish hairstyles. They also started to smoke and drink, and they took on new jobs like secretary, teacher, and telephone operator.
    • Sports and Leisure
      • Movie theaters became more popular.
      • Radio had play-by-play descriptions of sports games.
      • Sports figures were popularized.
    • The Prohibition Era was a time where the selling, manufacturing, and transporting alcohol was made illegal.
      • Bootlegging: the illegal making and selling of alcohol
      • Speakeasies: illegal places made for people to buy and drink alcohol
    • The Stock Market Crash of 1929
      • Stocks represent a share in ownership of a company. Profits increase when a company is doing well and they decrease when a company is failing.
      • The Stock Exchange is a public market where stocks are sold and bought between brokers (buyers) and traders.
      • A run on a bank is when a large number of customers of a bank withdraw their deposits simultaneously over concerns of the bank’s ability to pay one’s debts.
      • Causes:
        • Stocks cost money
        • The farming industry was overproducing
        • Less communication with trading
        • No knowing stock actual value
        • President giving false promises
        • People are buy stocks on credit
        • Mass production → overproduction
      • Effects:
        • Increase in unemployment
        • Decrease in consumption
        • Production, prices, and personal income are failing
